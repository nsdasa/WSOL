Lesson 1: Grammar and Guidance

  1. Introduction: This language is known as Cebuano or Bisaya. It originates from the tribe of Cebu, centered in the area that is now the city of Cebu. However, due to immigration and historical influences, the use of that language has spread with multiple dialects from central Philippines all the way to the Southern end of Mindanao. It is the 2nd most spoken language in the Philippines. The formal version centered in Cebu City is called “Cebuano”. But all together the dialects are called “Bisaya”. The dialects are very similar to each other. The speaker of one dialect can generally be understood by other Bisaya speakers. You will be learning the Bisaya dialect of Northern Mindanao. It has over 90% commonality with formal Cebuano.
  2. Basic Sentence Structure: While sentence structure can vary significantly, in each language there is a core sentence structure. In English this structure is Subject-Verb-Object (SVO). For example, “I went to the store.” Subject = “I”; Verb = “went”; Object = “store”. Various articles, linkers, prepositions, etc (e.g. “to” and “the”) serve as the framework to connect the Subject-Verb-Object. Of course, you can be like Yoda and say “To the store I went.” This would be an Object-Subject-Verb order (there are many languages that use this). But this is not the normal way that English speakers talk and if you talked this way you would sound strange and be difficult to understand. In the same way, when learning a language, it is very important to learn the simple “normal” way of speaking first.

In Bisaya, the normal sentence structure is Verb-Actor-Object-Location (or Referent).

For example:

“Nag-tudlo ko og bolpen sa lamesa” is literally:

“Pointing (nag-tudlo) I (ko) a (og) pen (bolpen) on the (sa) table (lamesa)”.

Verb                  Actor                   Object                         Location

As you learn more Bisaya you will find that just like in English, there are sentences that are structured differently. However, most of the time this VAOL is the pattern. Early on, almost all sentences we will use will be this pattern. So, push yourself to use the correct structure and lead with the Verb, then the actor (usually the subject of the sentence), then the object (if applicable), then the location (if applicable).

 

3. Introduction to Focuses: In English, we often use verbal emphasis to indicate the focus of a sentence. For example: If we want to emphasize the object of a sentence, we might say “I will point to my PEN”, verbally emphasizing the need to get the Pen (vs a Pencil), that also happens to belong to me. Alternatively, you might say, “I will point to MY pen” emphasizing the need to get the pen that is MINE (vs your pen). There are also more complex grammar constructions that English speakers can use to indicate the focus of a sentence. For example: “IT IS MY pen that I will point to” or “It is Me that will point to my pen” or “I will ONLY point to MY pen” or “I will point to my PEN ONLY.”

In Bisaya, however, the focus of the sentence is critical to the entire sentence structure and word usage. And they have a very effective and simple way of indicating this. Bisaya uses different verb affixes, pronoun forms, and articles to easily indicate the focus of a sentence without needing the use of verbal inflections or complex grammar constructions. Here is an introduction to the concept:

1. Actor: The focus of the sentence is on who is doing the action.

For example:

“Nag-tudlo ko og bolpen sa lamesa”

Pointing [am] I a pen on the table

“I am pointing to a pen on the table”

3. Location / Referent: The focus of the sentence is on the location or person / thing (Referent) that the action affects.

“Gi-tudlo nako og bolpen ang lamesa”

Pointing [am] I a pen on the table

It is a pen on the TABLE I am pointing to.

2. Object: The focus of the sentence is on the thing being acted upon, the object. For example:

“Gi-tudlo nimo ang bolpen sa lamesa”

Pointing [am] I the pen on the table

It is the PEN on the table I am pointing to.

Articles: An article is a grammar feature that helps connect words and indicate the type of noun. English has only three articles: a, an, the. However, Cebuano has 12 articles. For this lesson (and for a while) we will focus on the four most common articles: ang/si, og, and sa. These articles, except for “Si”, only have approximate English equivalents. Now, you might see in some books on Cebuano grammar that “ang” = “the” and “og” = “a” and “sa” = “to/from”, but you will quickly find that while this is somewhat true, it is not actually true. Quite often the article construction will just not really match the English. This is because the article usage in Bisaya is dictated by the Focus system (see “Introduction to Focuses”), which has no direct equivalent in English. In fact, you can clearly see this in action in the examples given above where both “sa” and “ang” can mean “a”, “the” and “on the” depending on the focus of the sentence. So lets talk about what these articles actually are doing.

Ang : This article goes before the noun that is the focus of the sentence. It functions similar to “the” in English. It also functions as a “definite” article, which means that it can be used to refer to a specific noun, not just any noun. But not exactly. Basically, the focus of the sentence will dictate what noun “ang” will go in front of. Look at the examples in the “Introduction to Focuses” to see this in action.

Og/Ug : This article goes before the noun that is the object. These days, most of the time it is spelled “ug”. But some still use “og”. The two are interchangeable. NOTE: “og/ug” also is used as the conjunction “and”. For example:

“Nag-kuha ko ug bolpen ug papel”

Getting [am] I a pen and paper

As the focus changes the location of the article changes . Note that if a pronoun is used, instead of using an article, the form of the pronoun is changed . Just like English, Pronouns do not get an article .

Affixes Everywhere: An “Affix” is an addition to a word that modifies, adds to, or changes the meaning of the root word. In English we have affixes such as “ed” (past tense) or “ing” (present tense) or “s/es” (plurals), “ly” (makes into an adjective), etc.

Most Filipino languages make much more extensive use of affixes than English. Affixes are used to modify verbs to indicate not only tense (e.g. past / present / future), but also focus (see “Introduction to Focuses” section), command form of the verb, indicatives (in English should, would, etc.), make verbs into nouns, nouns into verbs, and many other things. Affixes can even be stacked together to make more complex forms.

A good example of how affixes can change the meaning of a word is the word “tudlo”. This root means “finger”. But depending on the context of the sentence and the affix used, it also means “to point” or “to teach”. Literally the Bisaya for “point to the pencil” is “finger to the pencil”. And “teach Bisaya” is “finger to me Bisaya”. If you think about it, pointing and teaching involve significant use of the finger… so it’s a word picture (the technical explanation is that in its noun form the word tudlo is “finger”, it its transitive verb form it is “teach”, and it its ditransitive verb form it is “point”).

Linguists estimate that in Bisaya there may be over three-thousand possible affix combinations. However, about 50-60 are the most common forms used that can allow a speaker to communicate a majority of the Bisaya language.

The affix system is a major advantage of Bisaya and learning Bisaya. Why? Well, once you learn the root word (e.g. “sulat” = write or “basa” = read), and the affix system, you can take one root and like Legos attach affixes to make a huge number of variations of the word. This means that compared to other languages, learning Bisaya requires learning fewer total words! Good News!

Affixes in Bisaya mostly occur at the beginning (prefix) or the end of a word (suffix), but there are a few that you will learn much later that occur inside the word (infix). Sometimes the affix is written with a hyphen separating it from the root word, like “ nag- tudlo” . To help you learn, we will use the hyphen in most early lessons. But most of the time, in real world use, it is just added to the verb without a hyphen (e.g. “nag tudlo”) .

“Nag-kuha si Joy og bolpen”

Getting [is] Joy a pen”

In this lesson we will not be teaching most details of the affix system, but you should be aware of some affixes that will be used with verbs so that you can better accomplish this lesson:

a. “Nag-”: This affix is a prefix and is added to the start of the word (like “pre-” in prefix). It is “actor focus” in that it makes the sentence about the subject taking the action. It is also past/present continuous. Which means that the action has already started and may be still ongoing. Thus, it can refer to something that happened in the past and/or is still ongoing in the present. For example, the root word “tudlo” when used as nag-tudlo (or nagtudlo) it is the same as both “pointing/teaching” and “was pointing/teaching” in English. For example, “Nag-tudlo ko og bolpen” = “I am pointing at a pen” or “Nag-tudlo ko og Bisaya sa imo” = “I am teaching you Bisaya”.

6. Ain’t no “To Be” Verbs: In English, the “to be” verb is incredibly important to sentence construction. These words are: is, am, are, to be, will be, being, etc. You should know right now that there is NO direct equivalent in Bisaya (at more advanced levels you will learn of some approximate equivelents). For example: “I am pointing to a pen” is “Pointing (nag-tudlo) I (ko) a (og) pen (bolpen)”. You will notice that there is no “am”! That is because in Bisaya there is NO “to be” verbs. This can be challenging for English speakers. Your brain might really want to put an “am” in the sentence and trip up as you might think that the problem is that you haven’t learned a word for that. But NO. It does not exist. You might think that this makes sentences very confusing. But it does not. You just have not learned how to communicate without the “to be” verb… yet! Don’t worry, while “to be” verbs are great and useful, but you can totally have a language without them.
